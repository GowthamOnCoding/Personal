What is Anxiety?

The Brain-Body Connection

Anxiety is your body's natural alarm system, designed to keep you safe from danger.

  • Brain: Detects potential threats
  • Body: Prepares for action
  • Purpose: Survival mechanism

Normal anxiety helps us stay alert and perform better. Excessive anxiety can interfere with daily life.

→

Brain signals body to prepare for action

Speaker Notes:

Start by normalizing anxiety - it's a universal human experience. Explain that anxiety becomes problematic when it's disproportionate to the actual threat or interferes with daily functioning. Use relatable examples like feeling nervous before an exam (normal) vs. being unable to sleep for weeks due to health worries (excessive).

Health Anxiety: A Closer Look

What is Health Anxiety?

Persistent worry about having or developing a serious illness, despite medical reassurance.

Key Characteristics:

  • Excessive focus on body sensations
  • Frequent self-checking behaviors
  • Seeking constant medical reassurance
  • Avoiding health-related information or medical care
  • Misinterpreting normal body sensations

📊 By the Numbers

  • 🔹 Affects 3-12% of population
  • 🔹 Often begins in early adulthood
  • 🔹 Equally affects all genders
  • 🔹 Can be triggered by life events

💡 Important Note

Health anxiety is not "just worrying" - it's a real condition that can significantly impact quality of life.

Speaker Notes:

Validate the audience's experiences - many people have felt health anxiety, especially during times of stress or after hearing about illness. Distinguish between normal health concerns (which everyone has) and persistent, intrusive worries that interfere with daily life. Mention that in our digital age, "Dr. Google" often amplifies these concerns.

How Health Anxiety Starts: Sarah's Story

📖 A Real-Life Example

Sarah, 22, College Student:

"It started during finals week. I felt my heart racing while studying and immediately thought 'heart attack.' Even though I'm young and healthy, I couldn't shake the fear. I started checking my pulse constantly, researching heart conditions online, and visiting the campus health center weekly. The more I searched, the more convinced I became that something was seriously wrong. Sleep became impossible, and I couldn't concentrate on anything except my health fears."

⚡ Common Triggers

  • Stress (exams, work, relationships)
  • Normal body sensations during stress
  • Health information online or in media
  • Friend or family member becoming ill
  • Life transitions or changes

🔄 The Escalation Pattern

  1. Notice body sensation
  2. Worry about what it means
  3. Search for information online
  4. Find scary possibilities
  5. Anxiety increases
  6. Body sensations worsen
  7. Cycle repeats and intensifies

Speaker Notes:

Use Sarah's story to show how health anxiety can develop gradually from normal stress responses. Emphasize that Sarah's initial heart racing was likely due to caffeine, stress, or lack of sleep - completely normal during finals. Point out how the internet search reinforced her fears rather than reassured her. Ask if anyone can relate to this pattern of escalation.

The Fight or Flight Response

Your body's ancient survival system in the modern world

⚔️ FIGHT Response

  • Muscle tension
  • Clenched jaw/fists
  • Anger or irritability
  • Feeling "on edge"
  • Need to take action

🏃‍♀️ FLIGHT Response

  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Sweating
  • Shallow breathing
  • Dizziness
  • Urge to escape

🧠 What's Happening in Your Body:

Stress Hormones: Adrenaline and cortisol flood your system

Heart Rate: Increases to pump blood to muscles

Breathing: Becomes rapid to get more oxygen

Muscles: Tense up, ready for action

Digestion: Slows down (not needed for survival)

Focus: Narrows to perceived threat

The Problem: Your brain can't tell the difference between a real tiger and the fear of having a serious illness. The response is the same!

Speaker Notes:

Explain that these physical sensations are not dangerous - they're your body trying to protect you. In health anxiety, people often misinterpret these normal stress responses as signs of illness, which creates more anxiety. Give the example: racing heart from anxiety gets interpreted as heart problems, which causes more anxiety and more racing heart. Emphasize that understanding this can be incredibly reassuring.

🎯 Behavioral Symptoms

Excessive online health searches
Body checking behaviors
Avoidance of health information
Seeking multiple medical opinions
Social withdrawal

Speaker Notes:

Mention that experiencing some of these symptoms doesn't automatically mean someone has health anxiety - context and severity matter. However, if several symptoms persist and interfere with daily life, it may be worth discussing with a healthcare professional. Validate that these symptoms are real and distressing, not "all in their head."

Effective Coping Strategies

🧠 Cognitive Behavioral Techniques (CBT)

THOUGHTS
"I'm having a heart attack"
FEELINGS
Fear, Panic
BEHAVIORS
Check pulse, Google symptoms

🎯 Practical Techniques

  • Thought Challenging: "Is this thought realistic? What evidence do I have?"
  • Breathing Exercises: 4-7-8 technique (inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8)
  • Grounding: 5-4-3-2-1 method (5 things you see, 4 you hear, etc.)
