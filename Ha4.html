What is Anxiety?

The Brain-Body Connection

Anxiety is your body's natural alarm system, designed to keep you safe from danger.

  • Brain: Detects potential threats
  • Body: Prepares for action
  • Purpose: Survival mechanism

Normal anxiety helps us stay alert and perform better. Excessive anxiety can interfere with daily life.

"Anxiety is not always the enemy – it's a signal, not a threat."

💡 Think of it this way: "Anxiety is like your phone's notification: helpful when it warns you, but exhausting when it buzzes all day."

→

Brain signals body to prepare for action

Speaker Notes:

Start by normalizing anxiety - it's a universal human experience. Explain that anxiety becomes problematic when it's disproportionate to the actual threat or interferes with daily functioning. Use relatable examples like feeling nervous before an exam (normal) vs. being unable to sleep for weeks due to health worries (excessive).

Common Types of Anxiety Disorders

🌍 Generalized Anxiety

Excessive worry about everyday situations

e.g., "What if something bad happens at work?"

😰 Panic Disorder

Sudden, intense episodes of fear

e.g., Sudden breathlessness in a mall

👥 Social Anxiety

Fear of social situations and judgment

e.g., Avoiding presentations or parties

🏥 Health Anxiety

Excessive worry about health and illness

e.g., "This headache must be serious"

🕷️ Specific Phobias

Intense fear of specific objects/situations

e.g., Flying, spiders, elevators

🔄 OCD

Repetitive thoughts and compulsive behaviors

e.g., Checking your body 10 times a day

Remember: Many people experience multiple types of anxiety, and that's completely normal. Today we're focusing specifically on health anxiety.

Speaker Notes:

Briefly explain each type without going into too much detail. Emphasize that these often overlap and that having anxiety doesn't define a person. Mention that professional help is available for all types. Transition by noting that health anxiety has become increasingly common, especially with easy access to medical information online.

4/10

Health Anxiety: A Closer Look

What is Health Anxiety?

Persistent worry about having or developing a serious illness, despite medical reassurance.

Key Characteristics:

  • Excessive focus on body sensations
  • Frequent self-checking behaviors
  • Seeking constant medical reassurance
  • Avoiding health-related information or medical care
  • Misinterpreting normal body sensations

📊 By the Numbers

  • 🔹 Affects 3-12% of population
  • 🔹 Often begins in early adulthood
  • 🔹 Equally affects all genders
  • 🔹 Can be triggered by life events

💡 Important Note

Health anxiety is not "just worrying" - it's a real condition that can significantly impact quality of life.

🎯 Myth vs. Fact

❌ Myth: "If doctors say I'm fine, they might still be missing something"

✅ Fact: "Repeated normal tests are a strong sign you're okay."

Speaker Notes:

Validate the audience's experiences - many people have felt health anxiety, especially during times of stress or after hearing about illness. Distinguish between normal health concerns (which everyone has) and persistent, intrusive worries that interfere with daily life. Mention that in our digital age, "Dr. Google" often amplifies these concerns.

5/10

How Health Anxiety Starts: Sarah's Story

📖 A Real-Life Example

Sarah, 22, College Student:

"It started during finals week. I felt my heart racing while studying and immediately thought 'heart attack.' Even though I'm young and healthy, I couldn't shake the fear. I started checking my pulse constantly, researching heart conditions online, and visiting the campus health center weekly. The more I searched, the more convinced I became that something was seriously wrong. Sleep became impossible, and I couldn't concentrate on anything except my health fears."

"Even when my tests were clear, I couldn't stop Googling."

Arjun, 28, Tech Worker: "A colleague mentioned chest pain during a work call. That night, I felt a slight ache and spiraled into panic, convinced I was having the same issue. I spent hours researching symptoms online instead of sleeping."

⚡ Common Triggers

  • Stress (exams, work, relationships)
  • Normal body sensations during stress
  • Health information online or in media
  • Friend or family member becoming ill
  • Life transitions or changes

🔄 The Escalation Pattern

  1. Notice body sensation
  2. Worry about what it means
  3. Search for information online
  4. Find scary possibilities
  5. Anxiety increases
  6. Body sensations worsen
  7. Cycle repeats and intensifies

Speaker Notes:

Use Sarah's story to show how health anxiety can develop gradually from normal stress responses. Emphasize that Sarah's initial heart racing was likely due to caffeine, stress, or lack of sleep - completely normal during finals. Point out how the internet search reinforced her fears rather than reassured her. Ask if anyone can relate to this pattern of escalation.

💡 Breaking the Cycle

The good news: You can interrupt this cycle at any point! We'll explore specific techniques in the following slides.

🔄 Simple Real-Life Example:

The Loop: Chest discomfort → Think heart issue → Google symptoms → Panic more → Heart races → Back to step 1

🛑 The Interruption: Pause → Ground yourself → Observe, don't react

Speaker Notes:

Walk through each step of the cycle using Sarah's example: trigger (heart racing), negative thoughts (heart attack), physical symptoms (more racing, sweating), checking (pulse, online research), temporary relief (normal pulse), increased vigilance (constantly monitoring heart). Explain that each behavior meant to reduce anxiety actually feeds it. This visual helps people see that anxiety is predictable and therefore manageable.

7/10

The Fight or Flight Response

Your body's ancient survival system in the modern world

⚔️ FIGHT Response

  • Muscle tension
  • Clenched jaw/fists
  • Anger or irritability
  • Feeling "on edge"
  • Need to take action

🏃‍♀️ FLIGHT Response

  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Sweating
  • Shallow breathing
  • Dizziness
  • Urge to escape

🧠 What's Happening in Your Body:

Stress Hormones: Adrenaline and cortisol flood your system

Heart Rate: Increases to pump blood to muscles

Breathing: Becomes rapid to get more oxygen

Muscles: Tense up, ready for action

Digestion: Slows down (not needed for survival)

Focus: Narrows to perceived threat

The Problem: Your brain can't tell the difference between a real tiger and the fear of having a serious illness. The response is the same!

"Your brain thinks it's facing a tiger... but it's just an exam or a weird sensation."

❄️ Don't Forget: "Freeze" Response

Some people don't fight or run — they freeze or shut down. This can feel like numbness, disconnection, or being "stuck."

Speaker Notes:

Explain that these physical sensations are not dangerous - they're your body trying to protect you. In health anxiety, people often misinterpret these normal stress responses as signs of illness, which creates more anxiety. Give the example: racing heart from anxiety gets interpreted as heart problems, which causes more anxiety and more racing heart. Emphasize that understanding this can be incredibly reassuring.

Health Anxiety Symptoms Checklist

🧠 Mental/Emotional Symptoms

Persistent worry about illness
Fear of specific diseases
Catastrophic thinking
Difficulty concentrating
Constant health monitoring
Need for reassurance

💪 Physical Symptoms

Rapid heartbeat
Muscle tension
Sweating or chills
Digestive issues
Sleep problems
Fatigue

🎯 Behavioral Symptoms

Frequent doctor visits
Excessive online health searches
Body checking behaviors
Avoidance of health information
Seeking multiple medical opinions
Social withdrawal

⚖️ What's Common vs. Concerning

Normal: Rapid heartbeat after climbing stairs or during stress

May need evaluation: Rapid heartbeat + panic while at rest, lasting weeks

🆘 When to Seek Help

If symptoms last >6 months or significantly affect your daily life, work, or relationships, talk to a healthcare professional.

Speaker Notes:

Mention that experiencing some of these symptoms doesn't automatically mean someone has health anxiety - context and severity matter. However, if several symptoms persist and interfere with daily life, it may be worth discussing with a healthcare professional. Validate that these symptoms are real and distressing, not "all in their head."

Effective Coping Strategies

🧠 Cognitive Behavioral Techniques (CBT)

THOUGHTS
"I'm having a heart attack"
FEELINGS
Fear, Panic
BEHAVIORS
Check pulse, Google symptoms

🎯 Immediate Tools

  • Thought Challenging: "Is this thought realistic? What evidence do I have?"
  • Breathing Exercises: 4-7-8 technique (inhale 4, hold 7, exhale 8)
  • Grounding: 5-4-3-2-1 method (5 things you see, 4 you hear, etc.)
  • Journaling: Track thoughts, feelings, and triggers

💬 CBT Success Story

"I started writing down my fears and slowly saw the pattern – it wasn't my heart, it was my mind."

🌱 Long-term Lifestyle Changes

  • Regular Exercise: Reduces stress hormones naturally
  • Sleep Hygiene: 7-9 hours, consistent schedule
  • Limit Caffeine: Can mimic anxiety symptoms
  • Mindfulness/Meditation: 10 minutes daily
  • Social Support: Connect with friends and family

🏥 Professional Help Options

Therapy: CBT, ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy), or EMDR

Support Groups: Connect with others who understand

Medication: Sometimes helpful alongside therapy (consult healthcare provider)

Campus/Employee Resources: Often free or low-cost counseling available

⚠️ When tools alone don't work after consistent effort, seeking therapy is a wise next step.

Speaker Notes:

Emphasize that recovery is possible and that different strategies work for different people. The CBT triangle shows how changing one element (thoughts, feelings, or behaviors) can impact the others. Stress that professional help is not a sign of weakness - it's like getting help for any other health condition. Mention that many successful people have worked through anxiety with professional support.

10/10

Your Path Forward

🌟 Remember: You Are Not Alone

Health anxiety is treatable. With the right tools and support, you can learn to manage these worries and live a full, meaningful life.

💭 Imagine this: Waking up and not scanning your body first thing. That's possible.

📝 Your Action Steps

  1. Start Small: Practice one breathing technique today
  2. Track Patterns: Notice your triggers for one week
  3. Limit "Dr. Google": Set boundaries on health searches
  4. Build Support: Talk to one trusted person
  5. Seek Help: If symptoms persist, reach out to a professional

📞 Crisis Resources

Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741

National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988

Campus Counseling: Check your student services

❓ Questions & Discussion

Common Questions:

  • How do I know if my health concerns are "normal"?
  • What if I actually do have something wrong?
  • How long does it take to feel better?
  • Can health anxiety come back?
  • How do I explain this to family/friends?

Final Thought: Recovery isn't about never having health concerns again - it's about responding to them in a balanced, healthy way.

🚀 Your Gentle Challenge

Start today: One deep breath, one supportive message to yourself, one step away from anxiety.

💚 Thank you for taking this important step in understanding your mental health! 💚

Speaker Notes:

End on an encouraging note - emphasize that seeking information about mental health is already a positive step. Address common concerns: validate that it's normal to worry about health, but there's a difference between reasonable concern and debilitating anxiety. Recovery timeline varies but many people see improvement within weeks to months with proper support. Encourage questions and create a safe space for sharing. Remind everyone that resources are available and that taking care of mental health is just as important as physical health.
